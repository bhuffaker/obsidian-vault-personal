Yes — there are four jurisdictions with operational vacancy taxes long enough to evaluate empirically. The two with the most rigorous evidence are **France (1999)** and **Vancouver (2017)**. Oakland and Melbourne are useful but more limited.

## 1. France — Taxe sur les Logements Vacants (TLV), enacted 1999

The oldest comparable policy and the only one with a **peer-reviewed causal estimate**. Initially applied to urban areas >200,000 population with housing-supply imbalance; expanded 2006; reformed 2013.

**Mechanism:** Tiered rate on the cadastral rental value (potential annual rent), escalating with duration of vacancy. Currently 17% in year one, rising to 34% for longer vacancies, plus a 9% administrative surcharge.

**Empirical findings — Segú (2020),** _Journal of Public Economics_ vol. 185:

- **13% reduction in vacancy rates** between 1997 and 2001 (causal estimate via difference-in-differences combined with propensity score matching, using France's exhaustive administrative housing dataset 1995–2013).
- **Effect concentrated in long-term vacancies** (the policy target).
- **Most de-vacated units became primary residences** rather than rentals.
- **No statistically significant effect on new construction** in the medium term.
- **Effect approximately twice as large in highly populated municipalities** — i.e., where housing-supply imbalance was greatest.

An earlier Blossier (2012) PSM study using decennial census data found no effect, but Segú's access to annual fiscal microdata is methodologically superior.

[Segú (2020) — ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0047272719301409) · [Maxwell School working paper](https://www.maxwell.syr.edu/research/center-for-policy-research/events/webinar-series-on-property-tax-administration-and-design/papers/the-impact-of-taxing-vacancy-on-housing-markets-evidence-from-france)

## 2. Vancouver, BC — Empty Homes Tax (EHT), enacted 2017

The closest structural analogue to San Diego's Measure A: a municipal tax targeting non-primary, vacant residences. Rate started at 1% of assessed value (2017), raised to 1.25% (2020), and 3% (2021+).

**Outcome data (City of Vancouver Annual Reports):**

- Vacancy declarations: For the first time since the Empty Homes Tax (EHT) was introduced in 2017, the number of vacant homes in Vancouver has fallen below 1,000 as of the 2024 reporting year. The latest EHT Annual report shows a record-low vacancy rate of 0.49% (compared to 0.90% in 2017, when the EHT program was implemented in Vancouver). — roughly a **67% drop in declared vacancies over 7 years**, against a growing housing stock.
- Revenue: The EHT has also generated $194.3 million to support affordable housing in Vancouver since 2017. Unlike San Diego's proposal, Vancouver's revenue is statutorily earmarked for affordable housing.

**Independent causal evidence — C.D. Howe Institute (Caracciolo & Miglino, 2024)**, "Ripple Effects: The Impact of an Empty-Homes Tax on Canada's Housing Market":

- Difference-in-differences using neighborhoods within ~800m of Boundary Road, comparing Vancouver (with EHT) to adjacent Burnaby (without).
- We show that the tax effectively reduced the number of empty homes without impacting new housing construction. However, it did not affect average rent.
- Conclusion: **availability gains, no affordability gains.**

**Important confound to note:** residential vacancy rates are influenced by a wide range of factors beyond Vancouver's municipal EHT and the B.C. provincial government's similar Speculation and Vacancy Tax (SVT). Broader market conditions play a major role, including interest rates, construction activity, and overall rental demand. Vancouver also has the BC Speculation and Vacancy Tax (2018) and federal Underused Housing Tax (2022) layered on top, so isolating EHT's marginal effect is genuinely difficult.

[C.D. Howe report](https://cdhowe.org/publication/ripple-effects-impact-empty-homes-tax-housing-market/) · [City of Vancouver 2024 EHT Annual Report](https://vancouver.ca/news-calendar/eht-drives-housing-vacancies-record-low-dec-2025.aspx)

## 3. Oakland, CA — Measure W (Vacant Property Tax), enacted 2018

The closest US precedent — passed November 2018 with 70% voter approval (required 2/3 because it is a special tax with earmarked revenue, unlike SD's general-tax structure).

**Mechanism:** Flat $6,000/year on single-family vacant parcels; $3,000/year on condos, duplexes, townhomes, and ground-floor commercial parcels. Triggers at vacancy ≥50 days/year.

**Outcomes (Shute, Mihaly & Weinberger LLP analysis 2022 + Oaklandside reporting):**

- Year-1 revenue: In its first year, the tax brought in $5.6 million, which is earmarked for homelessness services and illegal dumping mitigation.
- The tax has generated a few million dollars a year in revenue and has contributed to a negligible uptick in housing availability. City officials note that the number of taxed, vacant parcels is trending downward, suggesting the levy is having some effect.
- **Implementation problems:** documented cases of unintended incidence — including nonprofits acquiring vacant lots specifically to build affordable housing being assessed under the tax (POOR Magazine case, 2021). Exemptions exist but require affirmative filing and case-by-case review.

[SMW Vacancy Tax analysis](https://www.smwlaw.com/2022/10/26/vacant-house-taxes-one-tool-to-ease-housing-pressures/) · [Oakland VPT page](https://www.oaklandca.gov/Business/For-Landlords/Vacant-Property-Tax-VPT)

## 4. Victoria, Australia — Vacant Residential Land Tax (VRLT), enacted 2018

Useful as a **cautionary case on enforcement and self-reporting reliance.**

**Mechanism:** 1% of Capital Improved Value (rising to 2% in Y2, 3% in Y3 of consecutive vacancy as of 2025 reform). Originally limited to 16 inner Melbourne LGAs; expanded statewide January 1, 2025.

**Effectiveness — Prosper Australia "Speculative Vacancies" reports:**

- Self-declaration uptake was extremely low — in 2018–19, ~587 properties were declared vacant against an estimated ~20,000+ properties using zero water and likely vacant; **~2.6% compliance rate** by Prosper's metric.
- Recent State Revenue Office investigations (2024–25) audited apartment towers and identified 177 liable properties in just 5 buildings — confirming systematic under-reporting.
- Hayton Kosky review (2025): With only 1,779 properties assessed in 2024 compared to an estimated 5,000 liable properties, the majority of vacant homes remain off the market. This enforcement failure perpetuates housing supply constraints at a time when Victoria faces acute housing shortages and affordability pressures.

This is the strongest data point in opponents' favor: when a vacancy tax relies on self-declaration without robust audit infrastructure, compliance and effect can be far below projections.

[Prosper Australia Speculative Vacancies](https://www.macrobusiness.com.au/2020/12/melbourne-blighted-by-69000-vacant-properties) · [Victoria SRO VRLT](https://www.sro.vic.gov.au/owning-property/vacant-residential-land-tax)

## Synthesis applicable to San Diego Measure A

|Question|What the evidence says|
|---|---|
|Does it reduce vacancy?|**Yes** — France 13%, Vancouver ~45% rate decline. Causal evidence is robust.|
|Does it raise rent or hurt construction?|**No** — both Segú and C.D. Howe find no adverse effects on rents or new supply.|
|Does it lower rents / improve affordability?|**No** — Vancouver's prices and rents remained high despite the EHT; ~5,100 affected units is too small a share of total housing stock to move prices.|
|Does projected revenue materialize?|**Partially** — Oakland came in within the projected range; Victoria fell far below projections due to enforcement failure.|
|Is enforcement the binding constraint?|**Yes** — France's success rests on its administrative housing database; Victoria's failure traces directly to self-reporting without audits. San Diego's IBA assumes 45–70% exemption uptake, so enforcement design will determine which IBA scenario obtains.|

**Bottom line for the SD policy debate:** the empirical literature mostly supports the _direction_ of the supporters' claims (vacancy reduction works, no negative spillovers to construction or rents) and mostly supports the _deflationary_ version of opponents' affordability arguments (vacancy taxes don't materially lower housing costs). The two genuinely open questions Measure A faces — distinct from the empirical record — are the **legal-defensibility** issue raised by SF Prop M and the **earmarking/general-fund** issue, neither of which the international evidence resolves.
